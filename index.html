🚨 Clozapine — Treatment-Refractory Only

Most effective SGA but causes agranulocytosis (~0.8%). Requires CBC/ANC before EVERY new prescription. Early warning: sudden fever, chills, sore throat, mouth ulcers.

LAIs slowly release medication into the bloodstream over 2–4 weeks. Used when patients have difficulty adhering to daily oral medication. Significantly reduces relapse rates.

Cultural & CYP450 Pharmacokinetics (Slide 39)
⚗️ Pharmacokinetic vs. Pharmacodynamic Interactions

Pharmacokinetic interactions — one drug changes the absorption, distribution, metabolism, or elimination of another → alters plasma concentration (e.g., carbamazepine induces CYP450 → lowers OCP levels).

Pharmacodynamic interactions — drugs act at the same or related receptor sites → synergistic (additive/enhanced) or antagonistic (reduced) effects WITHOUT changing plasma levels (e.g., SSRI + tramadol → serotonin syndrome via additive serotonergic activity).

⚠️ Polypharmacy in older adults = complex interactions requiring extra vigilance.

🌍 Cross-Cultural Psychopharmacology

Ethnic variations in drug response are influenced by BOTH genetic predisposition AND cultural beliefs about mental illness and pharmacotherapy. CYP450 enzyme variants affect metabolism. Asian patients often need lower doses of TCAs and lithium. African American patients: clozapine agranulocytosis more common. Cultural dietary practices affect MAOI safety (fermented foods, soy sauce).

Anticholinergic Side Effects — ABCD
Anti-dry-mouth (dry mouth) Blurred vision Constipation Drain (urinary retention)

Caused by TCAs and some antipsychotics blocking muscarinic ACh receptors. Add tachycardia and confusion in elderly. Anticholinergic delirium = severe cognitive impairment from combined anticholinergic load — especially dangerous in older adults.

SAFE-T Protocol (Slide 34)
📋 Suicide Assessment Five-Step Evaluation and Triage

A pocket card protocol for structured suicide risk assessment, triage, and documentation. Used by trained professionals. Provides frameworks for developing treatment plans responsive to the patient's risk level. Includes triage and documentation guidelines.

Inpatient Safety Procedures
  • 15-minute checks at irregular intervals (not predictable)
  • Remove all dangerous items: belts, cords, sharps, medications
  • Verbal or written no-suicide contract
  • 1:1 observation for highest-risk patients
  • Give only 1–3 day medication supply to outpatient at-risk patients
Postvention & PAS
TopicKey Points
PostventionBegins 24–72 hours after suicide death. Support for family, friends, other patients on unit, AND staff. Assess all unit patients immediately after a completed suicide on the unit.
Psychological PostmortemMultidisciplinary staff review — NOT blame session — to identify missed cues and improve future care
PAS — US StatesLegal in CA, CO, HI, OR, VT, WA (Death With Dignity laws). Requires terminal illness, decision-making capacity, two verbal + one written request, 15-day waiting period.
PAS — InternationalBelgium, Canada, Finland, Germany, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Switzerland. Euthanasia (physician-administered): Belgium, Colombia, Luxembourg, Netherlands.
⚠️ IS PATH WARM — Suicide Warning Signs

A mnemonic for recognizing acute suicide warning signs (used alongside SAFE-T):

  • Ideation — talking about wanting to die or kill oneself
  • Substance use — increased alcohol or drug use
  • Purposelessness — no reason for living, no sense of purpose
  • Anxiety — agitation, anxiety, recklessness
  • Trapped — feeling trapped or in unbearable pain
  • Hopelessness — no hope for the future
  • Withdrawal — withdrawing from friends, family, society
  • Anger — rage, seeking revenge, acting recklessly
  • Recklessness — engaging in risky activities seemingly without thinking
  • Mood changes — dramatic mood changes

🔑 IS PATH WARM complements SAFE-T — used for early recognition by anyone, while SAFE-T is a structured nursing/clinical assessment tool.

🔥 Kindling Theory — Why Early Treatment Matters

Kindling refers to the phenomenon where each bipolar episode makes the brain MORE susceptible to future episodes — episodes become more frequent, more severe, and harder to treat over time.

Think of it like a fire: each "spark" (episode) makes it easier to ignite the next one. Eventually, episodes can occur with minimal external triggers.

Clinical implications:

  • Early diagnosis and treatment is critical to slow kindling progression
  • Missing doses or stopping mood stabilizers → breakthrough episodes → more kindling
  • This is a key rationale for lifelong maintenance therapy even when patients feel well
  • Explains why late-diagnosed or undertreated bipolar disorder often has worse outcomes

🧠 CT-R — Recovery-Oriented Cognitive Therapy (Slide 33)

Targets distress and disturbance by correcting self-defeating beliefs. Goes beyond symptom reduction to restoring the patient's sense of identity, purpose, and connection to others. Part of evidence-based psychosocial therapies alongside Cognitive Remediation (specific learning activities to improve cognitive skills) and Social Skills Training.

🚨 Refeeding Syndrome

When starved patient begins receiving nutrition → insulin surges → drives phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium from blood into cells → serum levels crash → heart failure, arrhythmias, respiratory failure. Start nutrition VERY slowly; monitor electrolytes daily; replace aggressively.

Nursing Interventions — AN
  • Weigh daily: same time, same attire, after voiding, before eating
  • Observe/sit with patient during meals; supervise 1–2 hours after eating to prevent purging
  • Limited meal time (30 min)
  • Tube feedings if <75% of meal eaten or weight lost
  • Do NOT directly challenge body image — state that your perception differs
Medical Complications
AN: Bradycardia, prolonged QT, amenorrhea, osteoporosis, lanugo, refeeding syndrome
BN: Dental erosion, parotid enlargement, Russell's sign (knuckle callus), ↓K+, esophageal tears
